Hillary the Hiker

Thinks

Hillary thinks her chances of completing a thru-hike of the Appalachian Trail would increase if she knew how many miles to hike each day. She thinks doing a simple total mileage divided by days calculation doesn't take into account weather, terrain, and her health. For example, if she starts off too slow, she won't finish in time. If she starts off too fast, she may get injured and suffer prolonged downtime.

Sees

Hillary has seen other thru-hikers complete a thru-hike and she has seen others fail. The most common reasons for failing are running out of money and getting too severely injured. People often run out of money if their hike takes too long. And, people often get injured if they push themselves too hard.

Feels

Hillary feels her chances of success would increase if she was confident of the mileage per day per section was accurate. She does not feel good about her chances if she has to guess at her progress. She often asks herself - "am I going too slow? too fast?" Having confidence in a projection would boost her morale and increase her chances of success.

Does

Hillary often does shorter, section hikes of the AT and other trails. She has learned that it takes around 5 days for the aches and pains to level out and around 10 days for her to reach peak efficiency. She has learned that for each 1% increase in daily up/down elevation change, that shortens the distance by a similar amount. When the weather is wet and the trail is wet, her mileage per day is decreased by 20%. When the weather is below freezing, it requires an additional 1 hour per day preparing (e.g. heating food, putting on more layers) and that's 1 hour less hiking.
